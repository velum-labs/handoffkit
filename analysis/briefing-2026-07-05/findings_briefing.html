Product context — what we are trying to build

The product: a CLI where developers run coding tasks through ensembles of models — several cheap OSS models fan out in parallel, their answers are merged ("fused") into one response, and the user sees a single model. Users can also build their own ensembles or use our predefined ones, each with a documented strength (e.g. "best at repo bugfix").

The business claim we want to make: not "we beat every leaderboard" — frontier labs will always out-saturate benchmarks — but a Pareto claim: comparable or better coding performance at a fraction of the cost (score and dollars per solved task). Our panels are OSS-first (decision D8): closed models like gpt-5.5 are price anchors and baselines, not panel members.

The open engineering question: which OSS models to combine, for which kind of coding work, and whether our fusion pipeline actually converts theoretical headroom into real pass-rate gains. This briefing reports progress on that question after ~$34 of measured experiments.

What this briefing is not: a final launch claim, a benchmark submission, or a promise that fusion always wins. Several findings are deliberately negative ("don't fuse here", "public data can't rank panels") — those are product features, not failures.

Glossary — terms used throughout

Ensemble / panel
A set of K models (usually 2–4) that all attempt the same task. "Panel" = the members; "ensemble" = members plus the merging machinery.
Fusion / synthesis
How K candidate answers become one answer. Selection = a judge picks the best candidate. Synthesis = a judge reads all candidates and writes a new answer that can combine parts of several — our product does synthesis.
Pass rate / pass@1
Fraction of tasks where the model (or fused output) passes the benchmark's grader. On 60 tasks, 27 passes = 45.0%.
pp (percentage points)
Absolute difference between two rates. 45% vs 30% = +15pp, not "+50% relative". We always use pp for comparisons.
Oracle / panel ceiling
The score if, on every task, a perfect referee always chose a passing member whenever any member passed. Measures potential — nobody achieves the oracle in practice.
Headroom
Oracle minus the best single member's pass rate. If the best member alone gets 60% and the oracle is 72%, headroom is +12pp — the room fusion could add if errors were perfectly complementary.
Capture rate
How much headroom the actual fused pipeline realizes: (fused − best member) ÷ (oracle − best member). 50% capture on +12pp headroom ≈ +6pp over the best member. Can exceed 100% if synthesis combines partial answers into new passes the oracle didn't count.
Peer field vs lopsided
Peer = several models score close together → ensembles can help. Lopsided = one model dominates by 15–20+pp → honest answer is "use the single winner, don't fuse."
Layer-1 / public prior
Evidence mined from public benchmark dumps (SWE-bench submissions, LLMRouterBench, Terminal-Bench). Good for shortlisting and vetoing; not authoritative for final panel picks (see §3).
Calibration / Layer-CAL
Runs we execute ourselves: same tasks, our harness, our spend ledger. This is where launch-grade numbers come from.
Truncation
Model hit the completion token cap before finishing. Truncated answers often fail grading for reasons unrelated to ability. We refuse pass-rate claims when >10% of rows truncate.
Wilson CI / 95% CI
Confidence interval on a pass rate — the range we'd expect if we repeated the experiment. Narrow interval = more certainty; interval crossing zero = inconclusive difference.
Pre-registration
We wrote the test plan (models, metrics, pass/fail rules) before looking at results — prevents post-hoc rationalization.
Frontier / price anchor
A closed, expensive model (gpt-5.5, claude-opus) used to compare score and $/solve — not a panel member in our OSS-first strategy.

2Complementary errors are real — the raw material exists

Checkpoint C1 (public per-task data, three independent sources): the best 2–3 system panels beat their own best member's ceiling by a wide, statistically solid margin. Oracle headroom = extra tasks solvable if a perfect referee always picked a passing member.

How to interpret

Each bar is oracle headroom on a public benchmark: how many extra tasks a perfect panel referee could solve vs the best single system in that panel. All three bars are positive and statistically solid (CIs exclude zero). This does not mean our product already achieves those gains — it means the raw material for fusion exists when models make different mistakes on different tasks.

So what: if headroom were near zero everywhere, fusion would be a dead end. It is not. The open question is whether our synthesis pipeline captures that headroom (see §7).

3…but public data cannot pick the panel (settled, twice)

Checkpoints C2 + C2V: choose a panel on half the tasks, score it on the held-out half, against the dumb baseline "take the top-K models by average score." Complementarity-smart selection never reliably won — and lost outright on SWE-bench Verified. Tested with two selection objectives across 7 datasets.

How to interpret

Each row compares two panel-selection strategies on held-out tasks the selector never saw. The colored band is the 95% CI on the difference vs "just pick the top-K models by average score." The dark tick is the point estimate; the vertical line is zero (no difference).

Win = entire band sits to the right of zero (smart selection beats dumb baseline). That never happened in 14 pre-registered cases. loses = smart selection is worse out of sample. inconclusive = CI crosses zero — we cannot claim a win or loss.

So what: public per-task data is useful for shortlisting ("these models are plausible") and vetoing redundant pairs — not for final panel membership. Calibration runs (~$6–17 each) are mandatory (decision D2).

4Field shape decides whether to fuse — and OSS fields are peer-shaped

6Measurement integrity: the truncation ladder

Our single most repeated lesson: thinking-style models produce garbage numbers unless completion budgets are huge — and published benchmark scores rarely disclose this. We enforce: >10% truncated rows ⇒ pass rate refused.

How to interpret

Truncation = the model hit the completion token cap before finishing its answer. Truncated outputs often fail grading for mechanical reasons, not ability. We refuse pass-rate claims when >10% of rows truncate (decision D11).

7The wildcard: synthesis once beat the theoretical selection ceiling

In the original C3 replay, the fused answer stream scored above the frozen bank's oracle — impossible for any pick-the-best-candidate scheme, possible only if synthesis combines partial answers into new passing ones.

How to interpret

Best single member = highest pass rate any one panel model achieved alone. Oracle ceiling = theoretical max if a perfect judge always picked a passing member — no selection scheme can beat this. Fused output = what our synthesis engine actually produced.

Capture rate = (fused − best member) ÷ (oracle − best member). 150% means synthesis beat the oracle — only possible if the judge combines partial answers into new passes. Tagged directional because the replay used a fallback judge and same frozen panel outputs; capture-pilot-1 will test this cleanly on fresh items.

Why this is "directional", not proven: the replay used a GPT-5.5 fallback judge (the committed Kimi judge failed on a malformed response), and some uplift came from the judge synthesizing answers itself rather than pure selection. The pre-registered, leak-free protocol to test this cleanly is exactly what capture-pilot-1 (paused, 29/60 member rows done, $0.51 spent) is designed to run. If it replicates, the product story upgrades from "cheaper" to "better and cheaper" — the single highest-leverage open question in the program.

10Discussion questions for this meeting

How to use this section

These are decision points, not rhetorical prompts. Each maps to a fork in the launch plan with a default recommendation — but may need exec alignment on budget, timeline, or risk tolerance. Aim to leave with answers on launch shape (Q1), capture bet (Q2), and spend authority (Q6) at minimum.

  1. Launch shape: is v1 "predefined fused ensembles" (wait for C+E) or "honest routing + DIY ensembles now" (path D) with fusion as v1.1? What is the minimum evidence bar we're comfortable putting our name on?
  2. The capture bet: if pilot-1 shows capture ≥100% replicates, synthesis design becomes our moat — do we then invest in judge/synthesis R&D as a first-class workstream (prompts, multi-round synthesis, non-member judges)?
  3. Domain priority: repo bugfix has the best headroom+demand but needs harness work; terminal/agentic is second and partially covered by public infrastructure. Do we accept the engineering cost of B before any launch-grade number exists?
  4. Price anchor: which frontier model do we price against on the card — claude-opus-4.1 (the scan's anchor), gpt-5.5, or "cheapest frontier that beats our score"? This choice defines the $/solve headline.
  5. Kimi asset: "kimi-k2-thinking is unmeasurable single-shot below 64k" is a finding nobody else publishes. Do we use measurement-integrity results as public content (credibility play), or keep them internal?
  6. Spend authority: the next ~$65 (A + C) is decision-grade; Step 5 is $100–500. Approve the full funnel now, or gate at C's result?
